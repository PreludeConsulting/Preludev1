## Broad answers (use before mentor handoff)

### FAFSA & financial aid

- **FAFSA** = Free Application for Federal Student Aid. It is the main federal form families use to apply for grants, work-study, and federal loans.
- FAFSA typically opens **October 1** each year for the following academic year (confirm on studentaid.gov for the current cycle).
- It uses **prior-prior year** tax information (e.g., for 2026–27 aid, tax data from two years earlier is common — verify on the official site).
- Many colleges also require the **CSS Profile** for institutional aid; check each school’s financial aid page.
- **Merit scholarships** are often separate from FAFSA; deadlines and criteria vary by college and private programs.

### College lists (safety, target, reach)

- **Safety**: Your academics are stronger than the school’s typical admitted student; admission is likely but not guaranteed.
- **Target (match)**: Your profile is close to the middle of admitted students.
- **Reach**: Your profile is below the typical range or the school is highly selective; admission is possible but less likely.
- Build lists using **fit**: major, size, location, cost, culture, and support services — not rankings alone.

### Application timelines

- **Early Decision (ED)**: Usually binding — if admitted, you commit to attend (verify each college’s policy).
- **Early Action (EA)**: Usually non-binding early deadline; you may still apply elsewhere.
- **Regular Decision (RD)**: Standard winter deadlines (often January–February; varies by school).
- **Rolling admission**: Schools review as applications arrive until seats fill.
- Encourage students to track deadlines on each college’s admissions page and in the Common App.

### Essays (Common App)

- The Common App **personal statement** is often **up to 650 words** (confirm on commonapp.org).
- Strong essays usually show **reflection and specificity** — a clear moment, value, or growth — not a résumé list.
- **Supplemental essays** are school-specific; read each prompt carefully.
- Prelude mentors help with deeper story development and revision; you may explain structure and brainstorming at a high level.

### Majors & careers

- Students do **not** need a final major to apply to most colleges; many allow **undecided** entry or change later.
- Exploring interests through courses, activities, and talking to people in fields can narrow options.
- Some programs (e.g., nursing, engineering at some schools) require direct admission — check each college.

### Tests (SAT / ACT / test-optional)

- Many colleges are **test-optional** or **test-free**; policies change — check each school’s admissions site.
- When tests are considered, they are usually **one factor** alongside grades, rigor, essays, and activities.
- AP/IB/dual enrollment can show rigor; policies on credit vary by college.

### Transfer & international students

- **Transfers**: Requirements (credits, essays, deadlines) vary; use each college’s transfer admissions page.
- **International students**: May need English proficiency tests (e.g., TOEFL/IELTS) and visa-related steps — official guidance comes from the university and U.S. government sources, not Prelude.

### Parents

- Parents can support organization, deadlines, and financial planning while the **student’s voice** stays central in essays and interviews.
- Comparing **net price** (after grants/scholarships) matters more than sticker price alone.
